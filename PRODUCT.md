# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Solopreneurs and overwhelmed founders — people carrying the whole business in
their head, with no team to delegate the "people side" to. They're stretched
thin and protective of their attention. The handful of relationships that
actually move their business (an investor, a key advisor, a first big customer,
a mentor) get crowded out by everything louder and more urgent.

Their context when they arrive: skeptical, tired of tools that add work, and
quietly afraid of dropping a relationship that matters. The job to be done is
relief — a calm, private place to tend the few relationships their growth
depends on, without it feeling like one more dashboard to maintain.

## Product Purpose

Aspen is a private relationship-management app for the 10–20 professional
relationships that matter most. It exists to make tending those relationships
feel intentional and unhurried instead of anxious and ad-hoc — and to do it
privately, with data that stays on the user's device.

This repository is the **landing page** for Aspen (a fictional product). It is
also demo material for blog posts and video tutorials on AI-assisted design:
the committed `main` page is a deliberate "typical AI first pass," and the work
here is to show a vast, visible improvement in craft. The redesign does not have
to be flawless — it has to read, unmistakably, as a different and better class
of page than where it started.

## Brand Personality

**Chic ski-lodge welcome.** The name is the brief: Aspen the alpine resort town,
not aspen the tree. A guest walks in from the cold and finds warmth, low light,
good materials, and a room full of friendly faces. Quiet luxury — the brand
never raises its voice because it doesn't have to.

Three words: **warm, refined, calm.** The tone is hospitable and assured, like a
concierge who already knows your name. The aspen-grove metaphor still exists
underneath, but it stays implicit — at most a single considered touch of green.
Nature-as-theme is not the play; *place* and *hospitality* are.

## Anti-references

- **Loud HR / people-ops SaaS** — Lattice, Workday and that whole category.
  In-your-face, dense, corporate, conversion-funnel energy. Aspen is the
  opposite: personal, private, and quiet. If a section feels like it's selling
  to an HR department, it's wrong.
- **The current "first pass"** committed on `main`: gradient text, identical
  six-card grids, emoji icons, the pill eyebrow badge, numbered 1·2·3 steps,
  cream-into-white wash. These are the AI tells the redesign exists to escape.
- **Generic startup loud**: hero-metric templates, badge soup, "trusted by"
  logo walls, neon-on-black dev-tool cool. None of it fits a warm, private,
  anti-surveillance product.

## Design Principles

- **Walk into warmth, not into a pitch.** The first three seconds should feel
  like a welcome, not a value proposition. Hospitality over conversion tactics.
- **Quiet luxury.** Confidence is shown through restraint, material quality, and
  space — never through volume, badges, or shouting. Whisper.
- **The name does the work.** Lean on Aspen-the-place. Keep the grove metaphor a
  whisper; let "luxury alpine hospitality" set palette, type, and mood instead.
- **Calm for the overwhelmed.** The user arrives stretched thin. Every choice
  should lower their heart rate — generous space, soft contrast, nothing that
  demands. The page is relief, not another task.
- **Visibly better, honestly so.** As a teaching artifact, the redesign must read
  as a clear leap in craft from the first pass, without pretending to be a
  finished production product.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**: body text ≥4.5:1 against its background, large text
≥3:1, visible keyboard focus, and full keyboard navigation. The warm,
low-contrast "ski lodge" palette is exactly where AA matters most — soft tones
must still clear the bar, so push body ink toward the dark end rather than
settling for elegant-but-faint gray.

Every animation needs a `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` alternative (crossfade
or instant). Don't rely on the single green accent alone to carry meaning —
pair it with text or shape so the page works for color-blind users.
